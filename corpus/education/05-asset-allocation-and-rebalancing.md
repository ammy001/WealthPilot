# Asset Allocation and Rebalancing

## What asset allocation is
Asset allocation is how a portfolio is split across **asset classes** — most commonly equity (stocks/equity funds), debt (bonds, fixed income), and sometimes gold or cash. It is widely regarded as one of the biggest drivers of a portfolio's long-term risk and return, more than the choice of individual securities.

## Why the mix matters
- **Equity** — higher long-term growth potential, higher short-term volatility.
- **Debt / fixed income** — steadier, lower returns; cushions equity falls.
- **Gold** — often moves differently from equities; sometimes used as a diversifier and inflation hedge.
- **Cash** — safest and most liquid, but loses value to inflation over time.

The "right" mix depends on an investor's **risk tolerance, goals, and time horizon** — not on any single universal formula.

## Rebalancing
Over time, market movements drift a portfolio away from its target mix — for example, after a strong equity rally, equity may grow from a target 60% to 70%. **Rebalancing** means periodically buying and selling to return to the target allocation. It enforces a disciplined "trim what has risen, add to what has lagged" approach and keeps risk near the intended level.

A rebalancing calculation simply takes the current allocation, applies the proposed change (e.g. moving an amount from debt to equity), and shows the resulting new percentages — it describes the outcome, it does not recommend making the move.
