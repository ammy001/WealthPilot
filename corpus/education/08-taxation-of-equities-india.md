# Taxation of Equities in India

Gains and income from listed equities are taxed differently depending on how long a holding is kept and the type of return. The rules below are **general educational summaries** — thresholds and rates are set by the government, change from time to time in the Union Budget, and depend on an individual's overall situation. Official sources (the Income Tax Department and a qualified tax professional) are the definitive references.

## Capital gains: short-term vs long-term
For listed shares and equity mutual funds, the **holding period** decides the category:
- **Short-term capital gain (STCG)** arises when the holding period is one year or less.
- **Long-term capital gain (LTCG)** arises when the holding is kept for more than one year.

The two categories are taxed under separate provisions, and long-term gains have historically carried a threshold below which they are not taxed. Because the specific rates and exemption limits are revised periodically, the current figures should always be checked against official notifications rather than assumed.

## Dividends
Dividends paid by Indian companies are, under the current framework, taxable in the hands of the investor and added to total income. Companies also deduct tax at source (TDS) above a specified dividend amount. The net effect depends on the investor's applicable income slab.

## Why holding period and records matter descriptively
Because the STCG/LTCG line turns on time held, the purchase and sale dates and prices determine how a gain is classified and computed. Keeping contract notes and statements makes that classification straightforward at filing time. Fund fees (the expense ratio) are separate from tax and are deducted within the NAV, not at sale.

Tax rules are complex and individual-specific; this note explains concepts only.
