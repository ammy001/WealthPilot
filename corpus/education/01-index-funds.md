# Index Funds and Passive Investing

## What an index fund is
An index fund is a mutual fund (or ETF) that aims to **replicate a market index** rather than beat it. A Nifty 100 index fund, for example, holds the same 100 companies as the Nifty 100 index, in roughly the same weights. When the index moves, the fund moves with it. This is called **passive investing** — there is no fund manager picking stocks to outperform.

## How it differs from active funds
- **Active funds** employ a manager who selects stocks trying to beat a benchmark. They charge higher fees and may outperform or underperform.
- **Index (passive) funds** simply track the benchmark. They charge much lower fees and deliver close to the index return, minus a small cost.

## Tracking error
No index fund matches its index perfectly. The small gap between the fund's return and the index's return is the **tracking error**, caused by fund expenses, cash holdings, and rebalancing timing. A lower tracking error means the fund follows its index more faithfully.

## Why investors use them
Index funds offer broad diversification, low cost, and transparency in a single holding. They are commonly used as a **core, long-term** building block of a portfolio. Their return depends entirely on how the underlying index performs — they rise and fall with the market and carry full equity-market risk.
