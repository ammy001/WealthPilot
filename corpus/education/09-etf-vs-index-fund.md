# ETF vs Index Fund

An **index** itself is just a list — for example the NIFTY 100 — with rules for which companies it contains and how they are weighted. It is a measurement, not something an investor can buy directly. Two common products let investors track an index: **index funds** and **exchange-traded funds (ETFs)**. Both aim to mirror the same index; they differ mainly in how they are bought and priced.

## How each tracks an index
Both hold the index constituents (or a close representation) in roughly the index's weights, so their returns closely follow the benchmark before costs. The small gap between a fund's return and the index it follows is called **tracking difference**, and its day-to-day variability is **tracking error**. Expense ratio, cash holdings, and how quickly the fund adjusts to index changes all contribute to this gap.

## Buying, pricing, and NAV
- An **index fund** is bought from the fund house at its **NAV**, calculated once at the end of the day. Orders placed during the day transact at that single end-of-day price.
- An **ETF** trades on a stock exchange like a share, so its price moves through the day. That market price stays close to the ETF's underlying NAV through a creation/redemption mechanism used by large participants, but the two are not always identical.

## Liquidity and practical differences
An ETF requires a demat and trading account, and its ease of buying and selling depends on on-exchange **liquidity** — thinly traded ETFs can show wider gaps between buy and sell prices. An index fund needs no demat account and can accept automated recurring investments, but transacts only at NAV. Neither structure is inherently superior; they suit different ways of transacting.
