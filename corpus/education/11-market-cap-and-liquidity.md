# Market Cap and Liquidity

**Market capitalisation** ("market cap") is the total market value of a company's shares — its share price multiplied by the number of shares outstanding. It is a common way to describe a company's size, and it underlies how many Indian indices, including the NIFTY series, group and rank companies.

## Size bands: large, mid, and small cap
Indian market regulators define size bands by ranking all listed companies by market cap:
- **Large-cap:** the largest companies by market value (in the regulatory definition, roughly the top 100). The NIFTY 100 is built from this segment.
- **Mid-cap:** the next tier of companies below the large-cap group.
- **Small-cap:** companies ranked below the mid-cap group.

Larger companies are generally more widely followed and tend to show steadier price behaviour, while smaller companies can move more sharply in either direction. These are descriptive tendencies, not guarantees.

## Free float
Not every share is available for public trading. **Free float** is the portion of shares held by the public rather than by promoters, governments, or other locked-in holders. Most modern indices, including the NIFTY 100, weight companies by free-float market cap, so two companies with the same total market cap can carry different index weights if their float differs.

## Liquidity and why it matters
**Liquidity** describes how easily shares can be bought or sold without moving the price much. It is often gauged by trading **volume** and by the **bid-ask spread** — the gap between the highest buy price and lowest sell price. Highly liquid stocks, common among large caps, tend to have tight spreads; thinly traded stocks can have wide spreads, meaning transactions may execute at less predictable prices.
