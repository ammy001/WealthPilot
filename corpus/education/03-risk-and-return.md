# Risk and Return

## The core trade-off
In investing, **higher potential return generally comes with higher risk** — a wider range of possible outcomes, including losses. Lower-risk assets tend to offer steadier but smaller returns. No investment offers high returns with no risk; claims that suggest otherwise are a warning sign.

## What "risk" means in practice
Risk is usually described as **volatility** — how much an investment's value swings up and down. A common measure is standard deviation of returns: a higher figure means larger swings. **Beta** measures how much a stock moves relative to the overall market (beta above 1 = more volatile than the market, below 1 = less).

## Types of risk
- **Market risk** — the whole market falls (affects equities broadly).
- **Concentration risk** — too much in one stock or sector.
- **Liquidity risk** — difficulty selling without moving the price.
- **Inflation risk** — returns fail to keep pace with rising prices (a concern for very conservative holdings).

## Time horizon changes the picture
Equity values can fall sharply in the short term, but over long horizons markets have historically trended upward. A longer time horizon gives more room to recover from downturns, which is why time horizon is a key input when thinking about how much risk is appropriate.

## Return is never guaranteed
Past performance does not predict future results. Historical returns describe what happened, not what will happen.
