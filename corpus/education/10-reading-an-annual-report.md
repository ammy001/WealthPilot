# Reading an Annual Report

A company's **annual report** is the main document through which a listed company describes a financial year to its shareholders. It combines narrative sections with audited financial statements. Reading it descriptively means understanding what each part is meant to show, not judging whether the company is a good or bad investment.

## The three core financial statements
- **Profit & Loss (Income Statement):** revenue earned over the year, the expenses subtracted from it, and the profit that remains. It answers what the company earned and spent across the period.
- **Balance Sheet:** a snapshot on the last day of the year of what the company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the shareholders' equity in between.
- **Cash Flow Statement:** how cash actually moved, split into operating, investing, and financing activities. Profit and cash can differ because accounting recognises some items before cash changes hands.

## Narrative and assurance sections
- **Management Discussion & Analysis (MD&A):** management's own explanation of results, the business environment, risks, and operations in plain language.
- **Auditor's Report:** an independent auditor's opinion on whether the statements present a true and fair view. Terms like an "unqualified" opinion, or any noted qualification, indicate the auditor's level of assurance.
- **Notes to accounts:** detailed explanations behind the headline numbers, including accounting policies and breakdowns.

## Reading them together
The statements connect: profit flows into equity, cash flow reconciles profit to actual cash, and the notes explain the figures. Reading across all sections, alongside the MD&A and auditor's note, gives a fuller picture than any single number. Comparing the same items across years shows direction and consistency.
